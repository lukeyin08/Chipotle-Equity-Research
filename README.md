# Chipotle Mexican Grill (CMG) Equity Research

**Rating: Buy  |  Price target: $38 (about 24% upside)  |  As of June 2026**

A self-directed equity research project on Chipotle Mexican Grill (NYSE: CMG). It includes a fully linked three-statement model, a discounted cash flow (DCF) valuation, a comparable-companies analysis, a one-page research note, and a stock-pitch deck.

## Summary

Chipotle owns and operates all of its restaurants, earns about 21% return on invested capital, and has grown revenue at a double-digit rate for years. Over the year to June 2026 the stock fell about 42% on soft traffic (2025 same-store sales down 1.7%), leaving it near 19x EV/EBITDA and 26x forward earnings, below most fast-casual peers and its own ten-year average. This analysis argues the sell-off is overdone and values the shares at about $38, roughly 24% above the June 2026 price of $30.54, as traffic recovers, restaurant-level margins rebuild toward 27%, and the store count grows toward the company's target of about 7,000 North American restaurants.

## Approach

- Built the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow from Chipotle's reported figures (FY2023 to FY2025) and projected five years forward from an editable set of drivers.
- Valued the business with a DCF: a WACC build, unlevered free cash flow, and two terminal-value methods (Gordon growth and an exit EV/EBITDA multiple), plus two-way sensitivity tables.
- Cross-checked the DCF against a comparable-companies analysis using EV/EBITDA and forward P/E for fast-casual and restaurant peers (CAVA, Wingstop, Shake Shack, Texas Roadhouse, Sweetgreen).

## Key assumptions (base case)

- Same-store sales recover from -1.7% in 2025 to about +1.5%, then +3 to 3.5%.
- About 320 to 410 net new restaurants a year, toward roughly 7,000 in North America.
- Restaurant-level margin rebuilds from 25.4% toward about 27%; operating margin toward about 18%.
- WACC 9.4% (beta 0.98, equity risk premium 5.0%, risk-free rate 4.48%); terminal growth 3.0%; exit multiple 18x EV/EBITDA.
- Leases are treated as operating costs, so net debt excludes lease liabilities.
